# This Week in Lebo

The School Board revealed its final budget numbers this week, and the bottom line is this: a 3.5% tax increase—below the state-allowed maximum—with no dip into reserves and no staff cuts. For a $300,000 home, that's an extra $32 a month. But here's the catch: the district's tax base is shrinking even as home prices soar, thanks to a property assessment system so outdated that the average Mt. Lebanon home is now assessed at barely half its actual value. Meanwhile, the Mobility Board kicked off 2026 with new leadership and a looming question: what happens when the South Garage—Uptown's 300-space parking anchor—closes for demolition in the next two years?

# Deep Dive

## The Tax Math Behind the 3.5%

**The Numbers:** A 3.5% real estate tax increase will generate $2.9 million in new revenue for the School District in 2026-27. The new millage rate is 32.033, up from 30.950. For a $400,000 home, that's an extra $42 a month—$507 annually.

**The Hidden Detail:** The district could have gone higher. State law allowed a 4.1% increase this year, but the board chose to stay below the cap. More striking: this is the first budget in years that doesn't raid the fund balance to close the gap. After scraping bottom at 1.76% reserves in 2023-24, the district has clawed its way back to 5.99%—and expects to hit 6.61% by year's end. The goal is 8%, the recommended cushion for a district this size.

**What Happens Next:** The preliminary budget passes April 13. Final adoption is May 18, after the district receives certified property values from the County and calculates homestead exemptions. Between now and then, watch for the curriculum presentation on May 11—Social Studies K-12, English 6-12, and Math K-5 are all under review. Four new textbooks (including AP Human Geography and a new Honors Finance course) are up for public review starting April 7; you have 28 days to weigh in before the May vote.

## The Common Level Ratio Crisis No One's Talking About

**The Numbers:** Mt. Lebanon's Common Level Ratio—the measure of how closely property assessments match actual market values—has collapsed from 81.1% five years ago to 50.14% today. Translation: the average Mt. Lebanon home is now assessed at barely half what it's worth. Meanwhile, median home prices have jumped from $270,000 in 2016 to $410,000 in 2025—a 52% increase.

**The Hidden Detail:** This isn't just an accounting quirk. The district's taxable real estate base has shrunk four of the last five years, even as home prices soared. Only two other comparator districts—Baldwin-Whitehall and Bethel Park—saw similar declines. And unlike suburban districts still building new housing, Mt. Lebanon is landlocked. Dr. Freeze put it bluntly: "We're a 6x6 square island. There is no land for people to build on." The result: the district can't grow its tax base by adding properties, and falling assessments mean it collects less revenue per dollar of real estate value. The district has issued more tax refunds than any comparator district as of March 2026, with commercial appeals threatening to push refunds even higher.

**What Happens Next:** The only fix is a countywide reassessment—something the County Council has talked about for years but never voted on. Dr. Freeze publicly thanked County Councilman Gresback for bringing forward a reassessment resolution and urged the community to pressure Council to act. "We don't need people to talk about it," she said. "We need them to be actionable." If Council punts again, expect the School Board to face the same tax-base erosion next budget cycle—and the one after that.

## Special Ed Growth: 222 More Students in 10 Years

**The Numbers:** Special education enrollment has jumped from 11.3% of the student body in 2016-17 to 15.4% today—an increase of 222 students. Outside placements (students sent to specialized schools) have grown from 4.3% to 5.1%, adding 16 students. The cost for those placements ranges from $21,000 to $182,581 per student, not counting related services like personal care assistants, nursing, or speech therapy.

**The Hidden Detail:** The district now employs 28 more Personal Care Assistants than it did a decade ago—IEP-mandated staff who provide one-on-one support. That's the single largest staffing increase in any category, even as total district headcount rose by just 21 employees. Meanwhile, state and federal special ed funding has remained flat. Gifted education is up 18 students, 504 plans are up 230, and English Language Learners are up 50—all with minimal state support. The district receives just $37,000 in Title III funding to serve 214 ELL students.

**What Happens Next:** Dr. Freeze noted that COVID-era cohorts drove much of the recent growth—kids who went without services during remote learning and entered the system with greater needs. As those students age out, the district expects special ed numbers to stabilize. But the financial pressure isn't going away. The district is working to serve more students in-house rather than sending them to outside placements, but that requires staffing and space. The 2026-27 budget includes two anticipated Bradley Center placements at $43,200 per student—a fraction of the cost of the most expensive outside programs.

## Where Does the $2.1 Million Tech Budget Actually Go?

**The Numbers:** The district's total technology budget is $2.1 million. Of that, $620,000 goes to software and EdTech—but not all of it is classroom tools. Big-ticket items include Frontline (employee absence management) at $55,000, ProSoft (budget software) at $44,000, and Atlas (curriculum platform) at $24,000. Classroom software is cheaper: Blooket (world languages and science) costs $180 district-wide, Kahoot is $1,000, and Nearpod (social studies) runs $4,700.

**The Hidden Detail:** The $14,000 GoGuardian Parent contract became a flashpoint during the budget forum. It's the only platform that lets parents control their kids' school-issued Chromebooks outside school hours—but parents have to activate it. Dr. Freeze was blunt: "It's parental responsibility for home device usage. We provide the tool, but parents must use it." Board members pushed back on criticism of the learning management system (Schoology), noting that colleges and workplaces require students to navigate similar platforms. One board member who teaches at the college level called it essential preparation.

**What Happens Next:** The district's strategic plan includes a tech usage evaluation, with findings due in May 2026. Dr. Freeze emphasized that Mt. Lebanon's EdTech spending is lower than comparable districts, and the focus is on quality over quantity. Expect the May presentation to address questions about whether the district is getting value from its software investments—and whether some tools could be consolidated or cut.

## The South Garage Is Dying—What Comes Next?

**The Numbers:** The South Garage has 2-3 years of life left before it must be demolished. It currently holds 300 parking spaces, with 150 reserved for building tenants, staff, business owners, and visitors. The Municipality has hired CBRE, a national site design firm, to coordinate with developers on redevelopment plans. During the 1-2 year closure for reconstruction, the Municipality is considering shuttle services and temporary parking solutions.

**The Hidden Detail:** The Mobility Board spent most of its April 7 meeting brainstorming what the new garage should include: electric vehicle charging stations, bicycle parking, multimodal access. But the bigger question is what happens to Uptown parking during construction. The board didn't get into specifics, but losing 300 spaces—even temporarily—will ripple through the business district. The adjacent former Citizens Bank building is privately owned by a real estate investment trust, and the Municipality's attempts to coordinate have hit a wall due to the complex ownership structure.

**What Happens Next:** The Mobility Board is drafting a shared Google Doc to compile recommendations for the South Garage project. John Reagan, who has read the entire zoning code, will represent the board at an April 22 joint meeting with the Resiliency Board to discuss zoning impacts. The board also re-enacted its Capital Budget Subcommittee, which has about $30,000 in discretionary funds for mobility projects this year. Expect those funds to target quick-win improvements before the garage closure disrupts Uptown access.
