# Deep Dive

## **The Crossing Guard Who Wasn't There**

**The stakes:** 200 elementary school children crossing a state highway unsupervised because one person called in sick.

Beth's 8-year-old daughter wanted to walk home from school — exit the back door, cross Castle Shannon Boulevard at the staffed crossing, meet mom inside the library. Simple enough. Except the crossing guard, Tracy, was out sick. A police officer substituted, then got pulled away for a traffic accident. Beth's daughter nearly got hit. "If it can happen to my child, I know it can happen to anybody's child," Beth told the Mobility Board on March 3.

**The hidden detail:** Castle Shannon Boulevard is a PennDOT-owned road. Mt. Lebanon can't paint it, redesign it, or close it without state approval. The only safety mechanism is a person in a vest. When that person leaves, there is no backup plan. Chief Haberman acknowledged the incident and said the department investigated, but the crossing guard shortage is ongoing. The board floated a solution: apply for a "highway transfer turnback" — a mechanism where municipalities take back ownership of state roads. If Mt. Lebanon owned Castle Shannon Boulevard, it could redesign the crossing, install engineering solutions instead of relying on individual humans, and even close the road for farmers markets or outdoor concerts near the library.

**What happens next:** The Mobility Board is pushing for the municipality to explore the turnback option. But no one at the table could say who in Mt. Lebanon's government would lead that effort. The Manager wasn't present. The planner wasn't present. The consultant works for a private firm. "We need to change the narrative and inspire the community with one significant project," board member Beth said. "Nobody wants to walk around here because it's not fun."

## **Two Bans, Zero Recommendations**

**The numbers:** $45,000 — the cost for a single lawn care business to switch from gas-powered to electric leaf blowers.

The Resiliency Board spent months reviewing proposals from Lebo Green to ban plastic bags and gas-powered leaf blowers. Both would reduce litter, cut pollution, and shift the culture. Both are dead on arrival. The Sustainability & Local Ecology Subcommittee concluded Mt. Lebanon lacks the administrative capacity to enforce either ban. "I know of no one who says, 'Yay, I'm so happy to hear the leaf blowers again,'" one member said, but only one Pennsylvania community has banned them, and those that did offered businesses over $1,000 in incentives to switch. Mt. Lebanon has no department of sustainability. Greg Warden, the staff liaison, is half-time on sustainability issues. The Inspections Department is already stretched enforcing the new property maintenance ordinance. Who would check if a lawn crew's equipment is electric? Send inspectors into homes to feel if the blower is warm?

**The hidden detail:** The plastic bag ban faces a geographic nightmare. Castle Shannon's Shop & Save is in Mt. Lebanon. Mt. Lebanon shops are in Castle Shannon. Giant Eagle on Greentree Road is in Mt. Lebanon, but businesses across Cochran Road are in Scott Township. Most plastic bags littering Mt. Lebanon likely come from shopping trips to other communities with more robust retail. The subcommittee concluded a county or state ban would be far more effective than a patchwork municipal approach. Allegheny County has been exploring this for years.

**What happens next:** The committee won't bring formal recommendations to the Commission but offered to help promote voluntary reusable bag use. The Mt. Lebanon Partnership plans to set up a "need one, take one" reusable bag table at the Uptown farmers market. The board discussed creating a Mt. Lebanon-branded reusable bag, a decal for businesses that switch to sustainable packaging, and a directory of sustainable businesses promoted in Mt. Lebanon Magazine. As for leaf blowers, any board member can still make a motion independently — but no one did.

## **Your Zoning Code Is Getting a Two-Year Makeover**

**The timeline:** March 2026 through March 2027 — four phases, seven code modules, and a "wrap party" at the end.

The Planning Board kicked off a comprehensive zoning code rewrite this week, and consultant R.J. Susco from OHM Advisors promised to "force people to have fun with zoning." The current code is a mess — the sign section is buried somewhere residents can't find it, and the board routinely denies variance requests they wish they could approve. "The heart in me says, 'We should grant this,'" one board member said, "but looking at the law as written and the five requirements, we couldn't." People moved after being denied. The new code will reorganize everything: use charts, district-specific subchapters, and a separate section for signs so it "smacks you in the face when you open up the code."

**The hidden detail:** The consultant analyzed years of variance requests and found patterns — corner lot fencing (especially on busy streets where parents cite child safety), paper streets creating corner lot issues, parking space removal where homeowners want to expand, and sign ordinance mismatches where lot characteristics don't fit sign requirements. Staff will now create a spreadsheet marking requests that "would probably have been acceptable but hands were tied by current code." The new code will build in flexibility mechanisms to avoid forcing the board into denials they don't want to make.

**What happens next:** Phase 2 (the longest phase) starts now: roundtable sessions with the business community, focus groups with the Mt. Lebanon Partnership and Beverly Road Merchants Association, and diagnostic audits. The code will be drafted in seven modules, with steering committee check-ins every other module. A special edition of the "Inside Lebo" podcast is planned for late summer or fall. A community open house will follow (with a potential Uptown scavenger hunt for placemaking features). By 2027, the Commission will hold a final public hearing, and the municipality will debut new opportunities — potentially accessory dwelling units, urban chickens, or other uses currently prohibited. The project aligns with the Active Transportation Plan (recommendations due May 2026) and the Activate Uptown strategy.

## **The Library's $1,000 Gold Tuxedo Studs**

**The numbers:** $1,000 — the check from Treasure Hunt for a single jewelry donation; $16,000 — what non-residents spent on Canopy streaming service in 2025 (roughly half the total budget).

Silver, a Friends of the Library volunteer who handles jewelry and trinket donations, called Friends President Susan last week. "Are you sitting down?" she asked. Silver had discovered that a donated set of tuxedo studs was 14k and 24k gold. Treasure Hunt cut a $1,000 check. "She grew up in a family that owned a jewelry store," Susan told the Library Board on February 17. "She calls the library 'my happy place.'"

**The hidden detail:** The library is quietly facing a budget crisis driven by non-resident card holders. Of 593 Canopy users, at least 200 are non-Mt. Lebanon residents, accounting for roughly $6,000-$7,000 of the $16,250 spent in 2025. If Carnegie Library of Pittsburgh eliminates Hoopla (they're already capping daily borrowing after earlier limiting holds), 5,775 non-resident card holders could migrate to Mt. Lebanon's services. Worst-case scenario: the Canopy budget jumps from $20,000 to $137,000 annually, with half of that spent by non-residents. The library doesn't heavily promote Canopy or Hoopla Flex precisely because of this fear. Staff presented the analysis to the board but offered no immediate solution.

**What happens next:** The library is about to enter a 6-9-month construction project (likely starting Fall 2026) that will shut down the circulation office, the automated materials handler, the Mind Body Spirit area, and several meeting rooms. The Midsummer Reading Festival is cancelled. The Friends holiday sale can proceed, but artist vendors and musicians upstairs likely cannot. Director Robin warned the board: "Statistics are going to be all wonky, but you know, it's needed to move to the next phase for the library." The bond issue timeline is May 2026, with an 18-month completion window once the Keystone Grant is received (decision still pending from PA Department of Education). The only silver lining: the library has only 50 carpet squares left to patch the floors, and what's underneath is either 1990s carpet or bare concrete.
