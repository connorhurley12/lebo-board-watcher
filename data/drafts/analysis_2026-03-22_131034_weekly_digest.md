# This Week in Lebo

A light week at the dais, but a couple of items are worth your attention. The Financial Management Board is inching closer to a decision on whether Mt. Lebanon should run its own trash collection—a move that could cost $3.7 million a year but offer flexibility our current contract doesn't. The Parks and Recreation Board pushed back on a proposal to plant trees at the golf course, saying that land is earmarked for golf improvements, not food forests. And in a rare unanimous vote, the Zoning Hearing Board approved a 12-square-foot sign for Castlegate Green, the affordable housing development that's been occupied for two years but couldn't get a sign under our current ordinance. Next up: the board will review the Main Park Master Plan in April—a $35 million proposal that's been sitting on the shelf since January.

# Deep Dive

## The $3.7 Million Question: Should Lebo Pick Up Its Own Trash?

**The Numbers:** The Financial Management Board is zeroing in on a cost estimate for municipal trash collection: $3.7 million a year by 2029, assuming 13 new employees (two crew leaders, 11 laborers, and a superintendent), seven leased trucks, and a 40% fringe benefit rate driven by sky-high workers' comp insurance. That's $600,000 less than the projected 2028 contract renewal with our current hauler—but the $3.7 million doesn't include the cost of a storage facility ($1-2 million to buy industrial property), automated bins (over $1 million if we go that route), or the $36,000-$45,000 we currently pay for dumpster service at municipal buildings.

**The Hidden Detail:** The board spent significant time debating what you can't put a price on: flexibility. With municipal employees, we could respond to resident complaints, adjust service levels, and control whether we use bins or bags. With a private contract, "you have someone to blame it on the trash man," one board member said—"that actually might be worth it." The trade-off: municipal employees would earn roughly $100,000 a year in total compensation (salary plus benefits), far more than the $22-24/hour Waste Management advertises for haulers. Staff argued municipal workers would be "career employees," not "last shot job" workers, and could work laterally in other public works roles.

**What Happens Next:** The board will recommend hiring a waste management consultant ($30,000-$40,000) to run the numbers professionally—route optimization, labor cost benchmarking, and a break-even analysis that looks beyond year one. "They'll be able to say 'Well sure, year 1 it looks like this, but here's where you really get the benefits. It's not year one, it's not year two, it's year seven,'" one board member said. The board will draft a recommendation for the April meeting and deliver it to the Commission with multiple options based on priorities: cost, flexibility, or resident satisfaction. One wild card: the board noted that regional bidding through SHACOG is "really starting to fade" because few haulers want all-encompassing contracts—meaning Mt. Lebanon may need to bid independently regardless.

## Golf Course Hollow: No Room for Food Forests

**The Numbers:** The Resiliency Board proposed planting trees and creating a "food forest" in the hollow area at the golf course—a scaled-back version of an earlier plan that included hammocks, a pavilion, and trails. But Recreation Director David Donnellan told the Parks and Recreation Board that staff is "not in favor" for two reasons: the site is designated in the golf course master plan for a learning center (a chipping and short game practice area), and it may be needed for a large building to house and charge electric golf carts when the current gas cart lease expires in 2.5 years.

**The Hidden Detail:** Resident Rob Mackey of Royce Avenue raised a flag during public comment: the hollow is full of invasive species—privet, Bradford pear, bush honeysuckle, and poison hemlock. "Not mowing without other mitigation of the invasive species potential would be the wrong thing to do," he warned. He also noted that "free trees" aren't always the best value when you factor in planting, watering, fertilizing, and deer protection. Donnellan agreed, adding: "Trees aren't no maintenance. We had five trees down at the golf course. More trees means more leaves, more maintenance of the tree, and then ultimately removing trees when they fall down."

**What Happens Next:** The board didn't vote, but the consensus was clear: the golf course needs to focus on golf-related improvements. Donnellan said the proposal "might be great even in its first iteration in another park somewhere," and the board will respond to the Resiliency Board suggesting they look at other locations—possibly the "back 40" behind the pool in Main Park. Meanwhile, the electric cart study is moving forward. Staff is investigating utility costs, charging times, and fleet size (the current 34-cart fleet would need to grow because electric carts can't charge as fast as you can refuel gas carts). A consultant will likely be hired to run the numbers.

## Castlegate Green Finally Gets Its Sign

**The Numbers:** After two years of occupancy, the Zoning Hearing Board unanimously approved a 12-square-foot sign for Castlegate Green, the affordable housing development off Castle Gate. The sign will identify the project and state it complies with fair housing and equal opportunity—federal requirements tied to the Low-Income Housing Tax Credit (LIHTC) program that funded the development. The catch: Mt. Lebanon's sign ordinance only allows instructional/directional signs up to 12 square feet for uses approved as conditional use or special exception. Castlegate Green was approved as a "use by right," so technically, no sign is permitted under the current ordinance.

**The Hidden Detail:** The developer's architect, Douglas Develin, told the board this is the first time in 20 projects he's had trouble getting a sign approved. "I think it's just a little glitch in the ordinance that caught everybody by surprise," he said. The sign is required by the tax credit syndicators, who withhold final closing funds until all compliance requirements are met. Non-compliance could jeopardize the tax credits and lead to clawbacks, affecting rents. Develin also revealed that a brick seat wall near the entrance was partially built without permits about 1.5 years ago before the municipality stopped construction. The wall remains—it's not prohibited, just the sign mounting—and the developer could technically mount the sign on the wall by cutting the posts shorter and epoxying them to the top. One board member noted the irony: "Our first ever subsidized housing project" gets "a sign on two sticks" while nearby institutions like Brookdale and the Catholic high school have "big fancy signs."

**What Happens Next:** The developer now needs to obtain a sign permit from the municipality. The board approved the variance with the understanding that the sign could be mounted either on two aluminum posts (as proposed) or on the existing seat wall (which the Zoning Officer said would be "better in my opinion" to comply with height limits for directional signs). The alternative—putting 2-square-foot signs on each of the eight buildings—wouldn't work because the required information won't fit in 2 square feet, and it would preclude using building signs for their intended purpose (unit numbers and building identification).
