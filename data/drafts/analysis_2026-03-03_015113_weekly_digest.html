🏛️ The Deep Dive

The Uptown Glow-Up: $221K for Lights, Art, and Wayfinding — But It Won’t Last Long

$221,000 allocated by the Commission for Activate Uptown Phase 1 — but staff warn this “sounds like a lot” until you realize it’s barely enough to light up one plaza and one stairwell.

The plan: Turn the Clock Tower Plaza into an actual destination (not just a pass-through) with sun shades and artistic lighting. Uplight the barrel-vaulted stairway to the Parseway so it finally looks like the architectural feature it is (staff have wanted this for 15 years). Add gobo-projected crosswalks on the Parseway to guide train riders toward Uptown — because right now, there’s no clear path from the platform to Washington Road.

The Hidden Detail: The original plan to use gobo projectors on East End Brewing’s blank wall was scrapped. Why? Too low to the ground, too easy for kids to tamper with. Instead, they’re going with pin-mounted vinyl (think PRT bus wraps, but artistic). The consultant doing the work, Tim Steinhower, installs for major museums and Disney — this isn’t amateur hour.

The “Next Step” Prediction: Expect staff to ask the Commission for more money before 2026 ends. $221K will be “drawn down quickly,” and the goal is visible, noticeable improvements that signal more is coming — not just bike racks on the Parseway that nobody notices. If the first phase looks good, the Commission will likely fund Phase 2.

Plastic Bag Ban? Not Happening. Resiliency Board Says “We’re Not Ready” After Lebo Green Pitch

Lebo Green spent a year pushing for a plastic bag and container ban. The Resiliency Board’s Sustainability Committee spent months studying it. The verdict: No recommendation to the Commission.

The Numbers: Only Pittsburgh and Edgewood in Allegheny County have plastic bag bans. Only one other PA community was found with a ban. That’s it. Out of 130 municipalities in Allegheny County and 3,300 in Pennsylvania.

The Hidden Detail: The Board didn’t kill the idea because they hate the environment — they killed it because Mt. Lebanon doesn’t have the staff to enforce it. The inspections department is already stretched thin enforcing the new property maintenance ordinance. And the geography is a nightmare: Castle Shannon’s Shop & Save is in Mt. Lebanon. Mt. Lebanon shops are in Castle Shannon. Giant Eagle on Green Tree Road is in Mt. Lebanon, but businesses across Cochran Road are in Scott. Good luck explaining that to residents — or enforcing it.

The “Next Step” Prediction: Expect a resolution urging Allegheny County and the state to adopt a countywide or statewide ban instead. The Board called trying to do this “one piece at a time” across 130 municipalities “not particularly efficient.” They’ll support education and voluntary adoption (think Mt. Lebanon-branded reusable bags at the farmers market), but no enforcement.

Gas-Powered Leaf Blower Ban? Also Dead. Board Says “Nobody Knows How to Enforce It”

Same story, different noise ordinance. The Sustainability Committee looked at banning gas-powered leaf blowers. The answer: No recommendation.

The Cost: Communities with bans typically offer $100 incentives to homeowners and $1,000+ to businesses to switch to electric. One lawn care company estimated it would cost them $45,000 to switch (industrial-grade equipment plus multiple battery packs for all-day jobs).

The Hidden Detail: Even if you ban them, how do you enforce it? “Do we send people into other people’s homes to verify they in fact did have a running gas-powered leaf blower? Do you put your hand on it to see if it’s warm?” One Board member deadpanned: “I know of no one who says, ‘Yay, I’m so happy to hear the leaf blowers again.’” But enforcement is a joke. Police won’t do it. It’s not feasible.

The “Next Step” Prediction: Nothing happens. The Board noted only one PA community has a gas-powered leaf blower ban. Mt. Lebanon won’t be the second.

Hidden Hollow: The Golf Course Valley Nobody Sees Is About to Become a Nature Preserve

Hidden Hollow is a 110-foot drop from the Mt. Lebanon Golf Course clubhouse down into a valley along the 7th fairway. Most residents have never seen it — only golfers looking down from the course know it exists. It borders Castle Shannon, has wetlands and a stream, and was once farmland that never recovered. Public Works mows it twice a year. That’s about to stop.

The Plan: The Resiliency Board wants to turn it into a nature restoration site — replant native trees, add riparian plantings along the stream, and create two “food forest” areas (layered ecosystems from tree canopy to forest floor that support wildlife). They’re also proposing bat houses to reduce mosquitoes for golfers.

The Hidden Detail: This is not an active park. No trails, no parking lot expansion, no foot traffic. Why? Because errant golf balls pose a safety risk to pedestrians, and the only access is through the golf course parking lot (past the community gardens). The Board explicitly rejected making this a walking park because it would primarily serve Castle Shannon residents (who live closer) and create harvest-season concerns for the gardens. Instead, it’s a tree canopy expansion project — the Resiliency Board’s #1 mandate per the Comprehensive Plan.

The “Next Step” Prediction: The Parks & Recreation Board heard the pitch on February 18 and asked few questions (taken as a positive sign). Expect them to approve the concept at their March meeting, then staff will present to the Commission and apply for a Western Pennsylvania Conservancy grant (covers native trees, labor, and planting — zero municipal cost). The grant window is open through 2027, so there’s no rush. If approved, expect planting to start in 2027 or 2028.

PennDOT Is Finally Listening on Route 19 — But Don’t Expect Miracles

PennDOT District 11 is resurfacing Route 19 through Mt. Lebanon in a few years. For the first time in 15 years, they’re willing to talk about right-sizing Washington Road (narrowing travel lanes, adding bike lanes or turn lanes, improving pedestrian crossings).

The Numbers: 19,000-22,000 cars per day travel Washington Road in Mt. Lebanon. That’s a lot of traffic to squeeze into fewer lanes.

The Hidden Detail: In 2012, PennDOT sent Mt. Lebanon a “cease and desist” letter when the municipality proposed right-sizing. The state’s position back then: our job is to move cars safely and quickly — period. What changed? Mt. Lebanon and Dormont slowed the project timeline by several years (with help from Senator Dan Miller and Senator Fontana) to allow for better planning. And Mt. Lebanon now has a Complete Streets Policy — which legally compels PennDOT to consider pedestrian enhancements.

The “Next Step” Prediction: Expect improvements to focus on the commercial/congested areas (Uptown and Beverly Road) — similar to Dormont’s West Liberty Avenue project, where PennDOT added center turn lanes and bumpouts. Staff mentioned paint treatments and lane constriction as cheaper, equally effective alternatives to hardscape bumpouts. Bike lanes are a harder sell due to parallel parking and “dooring” risks. The first meeting is March 2, 2026 — it’s described as “a meeting for a meeting” — so this is years away from shovels in the ground.
