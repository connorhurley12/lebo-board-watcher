# Deep Dive

## **The South Garage Gamble: Finding a Developer Before the Clock Runs Out**

**The Numbers:** The South Garage has roughly five years of useful life remaining. The municipality is now launching a national search for a private developer to demolish and rebuild the site, aiming to have a plan locked in by 2028–2029 to minimize parking downtime. CBRE estimates the RFP process will take 9–12 months, leaving a narrow window before structural maintenance costs spiral or the garage becomes unsafe.

**The Hidden Detail:** The municipality holds an unusual advantage: site control. Unlike most public-private partnerships where the government must negotiate for land, Mt. Lebanon owns the garage outright. CBRE's Ann Rahm told the Commission this positions the project as a rare "world-class" opportunity for developers — but only if the numbers work. The biggest hurdle? Construction costs have "skyrocketed," creating financing gaps that kill deals even when municipalities offer tax increment financing (TIF) and other incentives. Rahm acknowledged this is "probably the biggest issue" for developers right now. One mitigation strategy: the municipality could build the parking structure itself using tax-exempt bonds (cheaper than a developer's taxable debt), then lease air rights above for residential or mixed-use development. Alternatively, a developer could build the entire structure and lease parking spaces back to the municipality, creating a revenue stream to secure financing. A third option: run the deal through the local Industrial Development Authority (IDA) to access tax-exempt bonds. The Commission also discussed requiring sustainability components — such as all-electric construction or LEED certification — as evaluation criteria. Rahm cited an Ann Arbor project where the city mandated all-electric buildings; major developer Hines responded positively, suggesting green standards don't automatically scare off serious bidders.

**What Happens Next:** The Commission will consider approving a contract with CBRE to manage the RFP process. If approved, Phase 1 (discovery) begins immediately: identifying the highest-and-best use for the site (workforce housing? market-rate apartments? senior living?), cataloging available incentives, and establishing an evaluation committee. Manager McIlvaine acknowledged the process might not yield a viable developer if the project "doesn't pencil" — but even a failed RFP provides valuable data to inform future municipal construction (for example, building a parking structure now that can support additional floors later if market conditions improve). The alternative to a private developer? The municipality builds its own parking structure, likely at higher cost but with full control over design, materials, and timeline. Either way, the Commission is racing against a 2029 deadline when the current garage's maintenance costs become untenable.

## **Pool Filtration: 90% Sure of a May Opening (If April Goes Well)**

**The Numbers:** The $1.3 million pool filtration replacement is on schedule despite a "brutal winter," according to Gateway Engineers. The contractor chipped through snow and ice to install a new 3,000-pound filter, a first-ever surge tank (to handle displaced water when swimmers enter), and replacement deck sections. The project must be completed by May 2, 2026 — opening day for the pool season. Gateway's Mr. Dudley estimated a "90% chance" of on-time completion, but cautioned that April will be a "sausage-making" month as the contractor finishes work while Pool Manager David Donellan preps the pool (welding, painting the tank, tile work on the deck). "A lot is going to happen in April," Dudley said.

**The Hidden Detail:** The project has already generated two change orders: a crane addition to help staff manage pumps more efficiently, and a storm drain relocation after crews discovered an unmapped drain that had to be rerouted around the massive new surge tank. A third change order is under review for potential pump and motor upgrades; a pool designer representative from St. Louis is visiting the site on March 24 to inspect the equipment. The surge tank itself is a first for the facility — an exterior concrete tank designed to prevent overflow when swimmers displace water by entering the pool. Fitting the 3,000-pound filter through the filtration room door was a logistical challenge, and the contractor worked through winter to stay on schedule. The real test comes in April, when the contractor and municipality crews must work "side by side" to finish construction and operational prep simultaneously.

**What Happens Next:** If the contractor completes work on time, the municipality takes over in May for final touches (painting, tile, landscaping). If delays occur — due to weather, change orders, or coordination issues — the opening could slip. Dudley's 90% confidence hinges on both the contractor's performance and the municipality's ability to finish its prep work in parallel. Residents should know by mid-April whether Memorial Day weekend swimmers will have a fully operational pool or a delayed opening.

## **Fire Engine Ordered Two Years Early to Beat Market Chaos**

**The Numbers:** The fire department is ordering a new Engine 1 replacement in March 2026 — two years ahead of its scheduled 2028 retirement — to beat 24-to-30-month delivery delays and rising costs. Chief Christophel told the Commission that manufacturers now require orders placed in early 2026 for mid-2028 delivery. The cost? Not disclosed in the transcript, but the Chief noted prices have "skyrocketed" due to market consolidation, steel tariffs, and mandatory NFPA compliance upgrades (complex electrical systems, seat belt sensors, computer tracking to prevent firefighters from sitting on unbuckled belts). Three companies — Rev Group, Oshkosh (which owns Pierce), and Rosenbauer — now control 75%+ of the fire apparatus market, prompting antitrust lawsuits from LA County, the State of Wisconsin, and the International Association of Firefighters.

**The Hidden Detail:** The fire department is implementing a standardization initiative to end decades of "reinventing the wheel" every five years. Historically, each new apparatus committee designed a custom engine, resulting in three engines with different manufacturers, pump locations (middle vs. rear), and storage layouts. Firefighters complained of "compartment slamming" — opening the wrong compartment to search for equipment during emergencies. In 2021–2022, Chief Sahada formed a committee to develop a consistent spec for all future purchases, seeking input from three manufacturers and adopting a "Toyota principle" of operational consistency (everything in the same place, reducing errors and streamlining training). The new spec also addresses Mt. Lebanon-specific challenges: tight turns on Academy Avenue, numerous cul-de-sacs with curbed circles. The department requires a custom cab (not a commercial dump-truck-style cab) to minimize wheelbase and avoid high-centering on turns.

**What Happens Next:** The Commission will consider approving the fire engine purchase contract. If approved, the order is placed immediately for mid-2028 delivery. The new engine will replace Engine 1 as the frontline apparatus, and the old Engine 1 will move to second-due status, continuing the department's 15-year replacement cycle (5 years first-due, 5 years second-due, 5 years reserve). The standardization initiative ensures future committees won't redesign the engine from scratch, saving time and reducing operational confusion.

## **Three Buildings, One Bid Package: Facade Repairs Bundled for Efficiency**

**The Numbers:** The municipality is bundling facade restoration work for three buildings — the South Garage (Levels 4 and 5), the Public Works building (northern facade), and the Public Safety Center (rear facade) — into a single bid package opening in March 2026. Combined estimated cost: $345,000 ($200,000 for the garage, $45,000 for Public Works, $100,000 base bid for the Safety Center with alternates for the north elevation, planters, and generator enclosure). Gateway Engineers is managing all three projects, which involve tuck pointing (chipping out deteriorated mortar 3/4" deep and replacing it), repairing cracked bricks, epoxy coating on exposed rebar, and caulking "skyward joints" (horizontal joints exposed to direct rain).

**The Hidden Detail:** The bundling strategy is a direct response to last year's pricing success. In 2025, the municipality planned to restore one facade at the Safety Center but received bids low enough to complete two facades instead, accelerating the four-year restoration plan. Gateway is hoping for a repeat in 2026 by leveraging scale: three similar projects at three locations attract competitive bids, reduce administrative overhead (one RFP, one contract award, one project manager), and allow contractors to schedule work efficiently. The South Garage work is part of Year 3 of a 5-year maintenance program to keep the structure safe until its replacement (see South Garage redevelopment story above). The Public Works restoration focuses on the old building section near the fuel station, not the newer construction. The Safety Center work targets the rear facade facing the parking lot, with optional alternates for the north elevation (facing the cemetery) and planter restoration.

**What Happens Next:** Bids open in March 2026. Gateway will present contract awards to the Commission at the April 14, 2026 meeting. If pricing comes in favorably again, the municipality may accelerate additional work (e.g., completing the Safety Center's north elevation or planter restoration this year instead of waiting). Summer 2026 construction is planned for all three sites, with coordination by Public Works Director Andrew's crew to maintain parking access at the South Garage during scaffolding work.
