## Municipally-Run Trash: The $6.2M Question

The Financial Management Board spent its January meeting dissecting whether Mt. Lebanon should operate its own trash collection when the current contract expires in a couple years. Staff estimates came in at **$6.2 million annually**‚Äîroughly 50% higher than the current $4-4.5 million contract. The breakdown: $2 million for seven trucks (likely leased), over $500,000/year in fuel (escalating to $600k over eight years), $900,000 in tipping fees, and $6.2 million in employee costs (including benefits, workers' comp, and PPE). Real estate is another headache: the municipality would need property for truck storage and washing (with environmental compliance for water runoff), but any discrete property search risks tipping off the press and inflating prices.

**The pivot:** After realizing in-house operations won't pencil out, the Board shifted focus to **regional cooperation**‚Äîpotentially partnering with Upper St. Clair or other South Hills communities to share executive costs and achieve scale. The challenge? Different collection schedules between towns. The Board will meet with Swissville (which recently went in-house) and refine cost models before drafting a feasibility report next quarter. One member noted bluntly: "If I call anyone about real estate, it's going to be in the paper."

**Why it matters:** If the Board recommends against municipally-run service, residents will continue under a private contract (likely with price increases). If they pursue a regional cooperative, you might see new collection days or shared equipment. Either way, this decision affects every household's weekly routine‚Äîand tax bill.

## Library's Two-Tier Card Dilemma: Access vs. Affordability

The Library Board is wrestling with a policy question that pits mission against money: Should Mt. Canopy operates on a pay-per-user model with tiered pricing‚Äîmore users push the library into higher cost brackets, and staff identified "super users" from as far as Peters Township (a different county).

**The debate:** Board members opposed to a two-tier system argued it conflicts with the library's "book sanctuary" image and opens the door to charging fees for other services. Supporters countered that Mt. Lebanon taxpayers fund the library at a high level, and other municipalities could choose to invest similarly. One member framed it starkly: "If it comes down to we are either going to lose these services or we're going to have to do this, I would vote to do it."

**Next steps:** Staff will present detailed usage data at the February meeting, showing exactly how many non-residents use these services and what the actual costs are. Director Robin noted: "I personally I'm not sure myself which way I would go. **Why it matters:** If the Board implements a two-tier system, non-Mt.
