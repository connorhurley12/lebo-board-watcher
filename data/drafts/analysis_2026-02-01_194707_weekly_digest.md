## **In-House Trash Collection: $6.2M Annual Operating Cost vs. $4.5M Current Contract**

**The Numbers:** The Financial Management Board's preliminary feasibility study estimates in-house trash collection would cost **$6.2 million annually** in operating expenses alone ‚Äî a **38% increase** over the current contracted rate of $4-4.5 million. This does not include capital costs (trucks, facility, equipment) or the risk buffer needed for redundancy.

**The Hidden Detail:** The board admitted a critical vulnerability: **"One employee call-out or one truck breakdown could shut down the entire operation."** Unlike legacy haulers (Republic Services, Waste Management) who can swap trucks and personnel across multiple municipalities, Mt. Lebanon would have zero backup. The board estimated needing **7 trucks** to run 3-5 routes daily, but would likely need to lease or purchase **8-9 trucks** to account for maintenance rotations and sick days ‚Äî similar to how police departments buy more cruisers than they deploy. This redundancy cost is embedded in the $6.2M estimate but was flagged as a structural weakness.

Additionally, **tipping fees alone run $900,000 annually**, and the board acknowledged that current invoices don't itemize tonnage ‚Äî making it hard to negotiate better rates as a one-off municipality. Legacy haulers own some disposal sites (vertical integration), giving them pricing leverage Mt. Lebanon won't have.

**The "Next Step" Prediction:** The board is interviewing Swiss Vale's DPW director (who recently went in-house) to validate assumptions and stress-test the $6.2M estimate. Unless that conversation reveals hidden savings or operational tricks, expect the Commission to conclude in Q1 2026 that in-house collection is **not financially feasible** and renew the private contract when it expires in a couple of years. ## **Library's Two-Tier Card System: A Clash Between Fiscal Reality and Mission**

**The Numbers:** Mt. Lebanon currently funds **9 e-resources** (Canopy, Hoopla Flex, America's Newspapers, Culture Grams, etc.) available to **anyone in Allegheny County** who holds an Mt. Lebanon library card. Canopy uses tiered pricing based on usage ‚Äî more users = higher costs. The library is concerned that if Carnegie Library of Pittsburgh discontinues county-wide Hoopla Flex funding (a real possibility), Mt. Lebanon's costs will spike and become unsustainable.

**The Hidden Detail:** The board's discomfort with the proposal is genuine and philosophical, not procedural. Director Robyn Vittek stated: *"I came up through large inner city libraries where I have a hard time saying these people can have these special things that these people can't."* One board member said: *"We're a book sanctuary, but sorry you can't use these."* Another warned: *"Once that door gets kicked in, man, does it start getting way easier to start saying now we charge for this and this and this."* 

The board is not split on facts ‚Äî they're split on **values**. They acknowledge the financial squeeze is real but fear the optics of exclusivity and the slippery slope of fees.

**The "Next Step" Prediction:** The board tabled a formal decision and asked staff (Sharon Binsky) to return in February with **detailed usage data, cost breakdowns, and a count of in-county vs. out-of-county cardholders**. An alternative is being explored: charging a modest annual fee ($4-5, similar to Ohio public libraries) for out-of-county residents rather than a hard restriction. Expect a policy vote by **May-July 2026**, likely landing on a hybrid approach (fee for non-residents, Mt. Lebanon residents get full access) rather than a complete two-tier system. The board's tone suggests they will not approve blanket exclusivity.
