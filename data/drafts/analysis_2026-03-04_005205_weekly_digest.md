# This Week in Lebo

The Commission signed off on $629,000 worth of street maintenance contracts this week—all under budget—while a consultant kicked off an 18-month overhaul of Mt. Lebanon's zoning code. The Resiliency Board decided *not* to recommend bans on plastic bags or gas-powered leaf blowers, citing enforcement headaches and cost concerns, but did advance a plan to plant hundreds of trees in a hidden valley at the golf course. Meanwhile, the Library Board is bracing for a construction-heavy 2026 that will shut down meeting rooms, force staff into makeshift offices, and cancel the Midsummer Reading Festival. And if you've been wondering why pickleball courts sat empty all winter: they're done, just waiting for 50-degree weather to cure the final color coating.

# Deep Dive

## **The Zoning Rewrite No One Asked For (But Everyone Will Notice)**

**The Numbers:** April 2027. That's when Mt. Lebanon's new zoning ordinance is expected to land after 18 months of drafting, workshops, and what consultant R.J. Susco promises will be "forced fun with zoning." The project will touch every corner of the municipality—from whether you can build a backyard cottage for your aging parents (accessory dwelling units, currently banned) to whether Uptown can keep its Art Deco soul as buildings turn over.

**The Hidden Detail:** The real story isn't what the new code will allow—it's what the *old* code has been blocking. A former Zoning Hearing Board member told the Planning Board last week that she watched residents get denied reasonable requests—like a fence to keep kids from running into traffic on Bower Hill Road—because the five-part variance test is rigid. "The heart in me says, 'We should grant this,'" she said. "But looking at the law as written... we couldn't. And I know that people would move after they were denied." The consultant is now combing through years of variance requests to find patterns where Mt. Lebanon's hands were tied by its own rules.

**What Happens Next:** OHM Advisors will spend the next seven months writing seven code modules (uses, districts, design standards, signs, etc.), checking in with a steering committee every other module. By fall, the first public open house will debut—along with a special edition of the *Inside Lebo* podcast and possibly a "zoning scavenger hunt" in Uptown. The draft will go to the Commission for statutory review in early 2027, with adoption targeted for spring. One quirk to watch: fencing rules for corner lots. Paper streets (undeveloped rights-of-way on old plats) currently force homeowners into corner-lot treatment, blocking backyard fences even when there's no actual street. The consultant called that one "weird."

## **The Library's Year of Living Dangerously**

**The Numbers:** Zero meeting rooms, zero Midsummer Reading Festival, zero Mind Body Spirit browsing area. That's what Library Director Robin is preparing staff for when Phase One of the library renovation kicks off this fall. The project—pending a state Keystone Grant decision and a May bond issue—will last six to nine months and require gutting much of the adult side. The automated materials handler (the machine that sorts your returns) will be offline, forcing staff to process books manually. Circulation will relocate to the Book Sanctuary. Storytime will move to the Learning Lab. And the 1990s carpet? When shelves get moved, you'll see either threadbare squares or bare concrete—the carpet was discontinued years ago, and only 50 replacement tiles remain.

**The Hidden Detail:** The real pain point isn't the construction—it's the e-resources budget. Library staff discovered that non-Mt. Lebanon residents with library cards are burning through half the Canopy streaming budget ($16,000 spent in 2025, with non-residents accounting for roughly $8,000). Worse, 5,775 non-residents currently use countywide Hoopla through Mt. Lebanon cards. If Carnegie Library of Pittsburgh tightens restrictions further—they're already turning away an estimated 500 requests daily due to spending caps—those users could migrate to Mt. Lebanon's Canopy. At $27 per user per month, that's a potential $137,000 annual hit. The library isn't promoting Canopy for exactly this reason.

**What Happens Next:** The bond issue goes to market in May, assuming the Commission approves. If the Keystone Grant comes through (decision pending), the library has an 18-month window starting April 1, but won't be "shovel ready" by then—architectural drawings and bidding still need to happen. Robin meets with Draw Collective this week to nail down timelines. Friends of the Library will lose their Holiday Sale artist/vendor fair space but plan to adapt. As Friends President Susan put it: "The world will still spin. And we will find another way."

## **The Contracts That Came In Under Budget**

**The Numbers:** $424,417.50 in street maintenance work for $13,045 less than budgeted. The Commission awarded four annual contracts last week: crack sealing ($13,000 to Russell Standard), brick street restoration ($89,955 to Seni), curb replacement ($153,587.50 to No Sweat), and root-damaged sidewalk repair ($167,875 to G. Pasqualian & Sons). Gateway Engineers split the bid advertisements to drive competition between concrete contractors, and it worked—the low bids came in 7% to 23% under budget.

**The Hidden Detail:** The brick street contract is a hedge against political regret. As one Commissioner put it during discussion: "Once they're wiped out, they'll never come back." Brick streets cost roughly three times as much to replace as asphalt, and the municipality has been chipping away at deterioration for years. Public Works Director Rudy Sukal confirmed they have enough red bricks stockpiled to tackle "a big chunk of Crystal [Street]" this year, where new curbs are already in place. If they move to another street, the contractor has itemized pricing for yellow and orange bricks. The crack sealing contract locks in flat pricing through 2028 with no annual increases—a multi-year deal designed to reduce rebidding costs.

**What Happens Next:** Crews will start as soon as weather breaks. The curb program targets streets ahead of this year's mill-and-overlay schedule, including wheelchair ramps and trip hazards. The root-damaged sidewalk program follows the municipality's 10-year phased neighborhood approach. And the brick streets? Crystal Drive gets priority, with low spots and dips addressed first. The Commission can award additional work up to the full budget amount if needed.

## **Hidden Hollow: The Golf Course's Secret Forest**

**The Numbers:** 110 feet. That's the elevation drop from the golf course clubhouse to the base of Hidden Hollow, a wooded valley off the 7th fairway that borders Castle Shannon. The Resiliency Board's Environment and Sustainability Subcommittee wants to plant hundreds of native trees there—silky dogwood, black cherry, oaks—and create two "food forest" areas that mimic natural ecosystems from canopy to forest floor. The catch: the municipality would stop mowing the upper grassy area (currently mowed twice a year) and let it revert to woodland. Cost to Mt. Lebanon? Potentially zero, if a Western Pennsylvania Conservancy grant comes through. The grant window runs through 2027.

**The Hidden Detail:** This isn't a park for Mt. Lebanon residents—it's a nature preserve that benefits Castle Shannon more than anyone. The site requires driving to the golf course lot (not walkable for most residents), and errant golf balls make pedestrian use unsafe. The Resiliency Board is pitching it as a tree canopy expansion project first, a golf course aesthetic upgrade second. Chair Chad Wade, who admits he plays golf "very poorly," argued that great courses like Augusta National and Torrey Pines are "not just challenging—they're beautiful." He also noted that bat houses (bats eat mosquitoes, which "really agitate when you're golfing") would be part of the volunteer-built habitat features.

**What Happens Next:** The Parks and Recreation Board heard the pitch February 18 and will discuss it at their March meeting. If they approve the concept, the Resiliency Board will present to the Commission at a discussion session. Staff will then apply to the Western Pennsylvania Conservancy's riparian tree grant program, with help from Tree Pittsburgh (where the presenter is a former board member with connections). The project is designed to require no burden on Public Works or Recreation—all volunteer-led. But first, the Commission has to sign off on letting a chunk of the golf course go wild.

## **The Snowplow Truck That Came With Everything (Except a Radio)**

**The Numbers:** $134,946.80 for a 2026 Ford F-550 4WD with a 6.7L turbo diesel, 6-speed automatic, 9-foot aluminum dump body, stainless steel salt spreader, and 9-foot plow. That's $13,053.20 under budget and $13,177 less than the next-lowest CoStars bid. The truck replaces a 2016 F-550 (Truck #225) that will be auctioned off once the new one arrives. The Commission approved the purchase unanimously, with one Commissioner joking, "Does it come with a radio?" (It does. And AC. "For business purposes only," the response came.)

**The Hidden Detail:** This purchase is part of a broader fleet efficiency push that saved the municipality $42,000 in fuel costs from 2023 to 2025—not by driving less (consumption actually rose 8%), but by locking in lower prices through CoStars rack pricing and running more efficient equipment. Public Works and Police are the top fuel consumers (24/7 patrol and snow plowing drive the numbers), but the Fire Department's usage is response-based, not patrol-based. Some fire inspections use full-size trucks because crews need to stay on-duty to respond to calls, while others use SUVs. The municipality tracks fuel consumption by vehicle to inform a Climate Action Plan due in 2030, per the Comprehensive Plan.

**What Happens Next:** The truck is ordered through Jim Shorkey Auto Group via the Pennsylvania CoStars purchasing program. Once it arrives, Truck #225 goes to auction. Public Works will add it to the rotation for the 2026-27 snow season. And no, Greg from Public Works won't be getting a radio upgrade—just a more fuel-efficient plow.
