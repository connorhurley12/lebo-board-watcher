# Deep Dive

## **Washington Road Gets 18 Parking Spaces — But Is That Enough?**

**The Numbers:** The Commission greenlit $270,000 (funded by Zamias) to stripe 18 parallel parking stalls on Washington Road between Bower Hill and Oakway. Duquesne Light will relocate power poles from the parking side to the opposite side of the street, where poles already exist. The result: two travel lanes, one parking lane, and a sidewalk.

**The Hidden Detail:** The design is functional — but one Commissioner called it "not the most inviting entry to the community. Quite the opposite." The stretch sits in front of an empty parcel that's been vacant for years, and the Commissioner pushed for extending Uptown's design philosophy here: more bumpouts for crosswalks, planters, protected parking, and street trees. "Our only priority here shouldn't be moving traffic," the Commissioner said. "It's an area where a lot of people walk as well." Staff agreed they could manipulate the design with additional bumpouts, but the Commission didn't vote to expand the scope.

**What Happens Next:** Duquesne Light is finalizing cost estimates (expected within 1-2 weeks). Once the municipality pays the invoice, Duquesne schedules pole relocation and the project moves forward. Whether the design gets more ambitious depends on whether Commissioners revisit the scope before construction — but for now, it's 18 stalls and a sidewalk.

## **Your 2026 Road Program: $2.8 Million, 2 Miles, and One Traffic-Calmed Street**

**The Numbers:** The reconstruction program budgeted $2.27 million; the base bid came in at $1.62 million (about $600,000 under budget). The street maintenance (mill-and-overlay) program budgeted $552,000; the base bid hit $462,000 (about $90,000 under). Expect 2-3 alternate streets to be added to each program.

**The Hidden Detail:** The municipality's Pavement Condition Index (PCI) has held steady at 78 for seven years — well above the 60 minimum required by ordinance. But that number comes from a 2021 inspection, and Gateway Engineers warned it's likely lower now. "I can pretty much assure you that the street maintenance numbers really aren't 70. They would be lower if we did a new inspection on those streets," said Matt Bagley. The new Violytics data (delayed this year by snow) will reveal which streets have deteriorated faster than expected. Meanwhile, utility companies have paved 18.6 miles (22% of all roadways) over the past seven years via street opening permits — another 2.9 miles are on the books. That's free paving for the municipality, but it also means the road program must coordinate with utility schedules.

**What Happens Next:** The base bid includes Cook Lane (partial concrete slab replacement where Columbia Gas already worked), Furwood Drive (continuing previous sections), Hoodridge Lane (a landlocked alley with a curb controversy — Commissioners questioned why it's getting asphalt wedge curbs instead of standard concrete roll curbs), Payton Drive, Pembroke Road, and Ruth Street (next to the school, requiring summer work). Alternates include Austin Avenue (where residents raised traffic concerns with the Mobility Board — the Commission may delay action pending a recommendation), Glades, Ordale (continuing last year's work), West End, and Terrace. The maintenance program includes Beverly Road (coordinating with utility work at North Meadowcroft), Broadmore (in front of the school), and **Scrubgrass Road with traffic calming** — converting one lane to parking, narrowing travel lanes, and adding a designated pedestrian area near the stairs to Old Hickory. Property owners are "really excited" and "very supportive," according to Gateway. The project includes delineator poles (2-foot-high anchored reflectors) to protect the pedestrian zone.

## **ICE Policy Gets a Public Restatement — And a Backstory**

**The Numbers:** Zero. Mt. Lebanon has not entered an agreement to work with Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE), does not ask residents' immigration status, and would only assist with crowd control in extreme scenarios. That's been policy for years — but most residents didn't know it.

**The Hidden Detail:** The Commission's resolution (R-7-26) came after a WESA story about another Allegheny County municipality where a police chief signed an agreement to work with ICE (noted as not legal). The story sparked anxiety in Mt. Lebanon — preschool teachers were asking what to do if ICE comes to their classrooms. The Civic Engagement Board flagged the issue, noting that while they could give "a very sophisticated answer" because of their role, "there's probably other organizations, institutions in the community that having a public statement would make a big difference in lowering that anxiety." The resolution also responds to an ICE statement about entering places of worship, which caused concern for preschools housed in churches and synagogues.

**What Happens Next:** The Commission passed the resolution unanimously, and President Seagler thanked "everybody for their work on this — a lot of hands were involved in putting this together." The Civic Engagement Board is now considering a proclamation or resolution to make the policies even clearer to the public. Expect more visibility for this policy in coming weeks, possibly through the Public Information Office or a community forum.

## **Scrubgrass Road Gets Traffic Calming — And Residents Are Thrilled**

**The Numbers:** Scrubgrass Road is part of the $552,000 street maintenance program, but it's getting separate funding for traffic calming. The project converts one lane to a parking lane, narrows travel lanes, and adds a designated pedestrian area near the stairs to Old Hickory.

**The Hidden Detail:** The pedestrian area (shown in pink on the plan) connects two existing sidewalks using delineator poles — 2-foot-high anchored reflectors that protect walkers from traffic. A crosswalk is already in place. Property owners are "really excited" and "very supportive," according to Gateway Engineers. Residents hoped for this last year but had to wait.

**What Happens Next:** The project moves forward as part of the 2026 maintenance program. Gateway is coordinating the traffic calming work with the mill-and-overlay schedule. Expect construction when school is out of session (like Pin Oak last year and Old Hickory the year before).

# The Smoke Detector

- **"Wait, What?" Financials:** The Commission approved a $201,183 lease-purchase for a Dodge 5500 bucket truck with aerial lift — a 7-year lease at $34,200/year, though the budget appropriation is $40,310. The truck replaces a 13-year-old vehicle per the scheduled replacement plan.

- **Curb Policy Debate:** Hoodridge Lane (a landlocked alley off Mt. Lebanon Boulevard) is getting asphalt wedge curbs instead of standard 18-inch concrete roll curbs. A Commissioner asked, "How would you know you're in Mount Lebanon?" Gateway Engineers justified the deviation by treating the alley as a "rear parcel" similar to Arland Way (done ~10 years ago). The alley is only 10-11 feet wide and doesn't meet municipal standards, but it's owned in fee by the municipality, school district, and Allegheny County — and the properties are landlocked without it.

- **Oil Index Warning:** Gateway Engineers warned that the PennDOT liquid fuels oil index — which has been dropping since 2023 — is expected to reverse and go up. If the index fluctuates more than 10% from bid time to completion, the municipality must pay the differential. "As we all know right now, it's going to be going the other way," said Matt Bagley.
